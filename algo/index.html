DETAILS
Neurons, cells inside the brain, communicate by sending electrical and chemical signals. Computational neuroscientists (also known as theoretical neuroscientists) try to understand this communication by using mathematical
equations
that model neurons as
inputs
and
outputs.
Inputs
send
go signals
;
outputs
process these signals and then relay the message to other outputs.

In this model,
two inputs
are connected to
one output.
The output waits for a
go
signal from the input. When this signal is received, the
output
becomes active and can pass the message along. But sometimes when the
input
says go, the
output
cannot hear it.

When neuroscientists are modeling the brain activity, they also have to think about the strength of
connections
between cells. The strength of a
connection
is called its weight.

When two cells have a good
connection
--a strong weight-- the output is more likely to
hear
the input. Persistence also matters. If the
input
says
Go!
over and over, the weight eventually increases--the
output
can hear better.

In this model, you can track the weights of two
connections
on the scoreboard. Try sending
Go
signals to the
output
. If the
output
cannot hear the signal, try to build a stronger
connection!

From the Outside In considers the perceptron: an algorithm that describes how our neurons process our environment - full of smell, touch, sight, sound and taste.
If we find something significant in all of these incoming senses, our neurons will try to pass a signal along to its network, communicating that we should pay attention to that smell - a whiff of smoke coming from down the hall, for example.
